Data mapping

Name Description
annual_inc The self-reported annual income provided by the borrower during registration.
home_ownership The home ownership status provided by the borrower during registration. Our values are: RENT, OWN, MORTGAGE, OTHER.
inq_last_6mths The number of inquiries in past 6 months (excluding auto and mortgage inquiries)
open_acc The number of open credit lines in the borrower’s credit file.
Remark:
- A credit line is a type of loan that allows an individual or business to borrow money and repay it, often on a revolving basis without applying for a new loan.
- A revolving credit is a credit line that remains available even as you pay the balance. Borrowers can access credit up to a certain amount and then have ongoing access to that amount of credit. Typically a credit card.
purpose A category provided by the borrower for the loan request.

Task 4. Bivariate EDA with ggplot2

Now we are looking for some obvious correlations or patterns of variables.

4.1 First impression with ggpairs()

GGally::ggpairs(
  myLCdata %>% 
    select(annual_inc, open_acc, purpose, home_ownership, inq_last_6mths))

In this ggpairs matrix we can see that the numeric variables (annual_inc, open_acc and inq_last_6mths) are heavily right-skewed. Furthermore these variables show extreme outliers, clustering most data points near zero.

Additionally we see that the linear correlations between these numeric variables are very weak. The “highest” correlation we can see is 0.151 between annual income and open accounts.

This suggests that we need to transform the data using a log scale.

4.2 Numeric vs. Categorical

ggplot(myLCdata, aes(x = home_ownership, y = annual_inc, fill = home_ownership)) +

In this boxplot we see a clear hierarchy in median annual income based on housing status. Borrowers with a ‘Other’ or ‘Any’ category demonstrate the highest median incomes. These are followed by those who have a ‘Mortage’ on their homes and the ones from the category ‘Own’. The ‘Rent’ and ‘None’ categories have the lowest median incomes.

The log scale emphasizes that the ‘Mortage’ and ‘Rent’ categories contain the highest concentration of extreme upper-income outliers. We see this because of how high the black dots stretch upwards. The points at the very top represent extremely high incomes in the millions.

We use geom_jitter to add tiny amounts of random noise to the placement of the dots. We also add a trend line to see if there is any relationship.

ggplot(myLCdata, aes(x = open_acc, y = inq_last_6mths)) +
  geom_jitter(alpha = 0.3, color = "purple", width = 0.25, height = 0.25) +

The plotted trend line indicates a slight positive relationship between a borrower’s number of open credit accounts and the number of credit inquiries they have made in past 6 months. This matches our insight from our ggpairs matrix in 4.1 where we saw that these two attributes have a weak correlation of 0.124.

Now this slight positive correlation means that individuals with a higher number of credit lines might be slightly more active in seeking new credit. But most borrowers have very few recent inquiries regardless of their total open accounts.

At the bottom we see the reversal from above, where ‘home_improvement’ loans are almost entirely taken out by mortage holders. This makes sense in our opinion, because their incentive to care for their immobilia is higher than the one for renters.

4.5 Adding a 3rd Variable

We will use facet_wrap to add an third categorical variable.

ggplot(myLCdata) + 
  geom_point(aes(x=annual_inc, y=inq_last_6mths, colour=home_ownership)) + 
  scale_x_log10() + 
  scale_y_log10() + 
  facet_wrap(~home_ownership) +
  labs(title="Distribution annual income and loan inquiries in the last 6 months by home ownership", x="Annual income (log)", y="Inquiries in the last 6 months (log)") + 
  guides(colour=FALSE)

The scatter plots display the distribution of recent credit inquiries against annual income divided by housing status. The vast majority of data points are concentrated in the mortgage, rent, and own categories. The remaining groups contain very few observations.

There is no visible correlation between annual income and the number of inquiries. Applicants across all income levels generally show a low inquiry count. Most data points cluster tightly between zero and three inquiries within an income range of 10000 to 100000.

The most extreme outliers belong to the rent category. The specific individuals with around 30 recent inquiries are renters. Applicants with a mortgage or outright homeownership rarely exceed a maximum of 10 inquiries.

The scatter plots illustrate the relationship between annual income and loan purpose. Debt consolidation and credit card refinancing are the most frequent loan categories across all income brackets.

Applicants with exceptionally high annual incomes predominantly seek loans for debt consolidation, home improvement, and credit card payments. The data points in these specific charts extend far to the right and well beyond the one million income mark.

Educational loans represent a very small portion of the overall dataset. A few applicants with incomes below 10000 do seek educational loans. Other individuals with incomes up to 100000 also apply for this loan type. This category is therefore not entirely exclusive to the lowest income bracket.

Categories like moving, vacations, and weddings are typically requested by individuals in lower to middle income ranges. The data points for these specific purposes rarely exceed an annual income of 200000.

Small business loans attract a wide distribution of incomes. This category features a notable outlier with roughly 30 recent inquiries at a lower income level. A similar extreme outlier exists in the moving category.
